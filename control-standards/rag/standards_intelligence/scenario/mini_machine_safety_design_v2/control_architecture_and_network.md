# Control Architecture and Network

## Architecture options

| Option                                   | Description                                                                                                                                       | Best fit                                        | Strengths                                                                                                          | Limits                                                                           |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| A. Standard PLC plus separate safety PLC | Standard PLC handles sequence, data logging, and diagnostics; safety PLC handles E-stop, guards, hydraulic trip, and spill or overfill trip paths | Recommended baseline for most industries        | Clear separation between safety and standard logic; good expandability; aligns well with local separation guidance | Higher cost and footprint                                                        |
| B. Integrated safety PLC                 | One controller family with standard and safety tasks partitioned by certified firmware                                                            | Good when platform standardization is important | Reduced panel space; easier tag integration; supports networked safety                                             | Requires strong governance for task partitioning and safety change control       |
| C. Safety relay-based minimal system     | Standard PLC plus discrete safety relays for E-stop and one or two guard or trip loops                                                            | Smallest low-complexity build                   | Simple, deterministic, low software burden                                                                         | Harder to scale; limited diagnostics; less suitable for many trips or remote I/O |

The local corpus supports the core separation principle behind Options A and B: safety-related control functions shall remain effective regardless of the state of the standard PLC or HMI. The local software standards guide now routes the linked software-lifecycle, PLC-language, and secure-development questions. ## Recommended baseline architecture

1. The machine shall use Option A unless project simplicity or platform standardization justifies Option B or C. [LOCAL: us/nfpa79/NFPA79_2024__Ch09__control_circuits_and_control_functions.md#3. Safety vs. Standard Control Separation]
2. The standard PLC shall own recipe, mode, trend, diagnostic, and historian tags only. Safety trips shall be generated in the safety controller or safety relay path. [LOCAL: us/nfpa79/NFPA79_2024__Ch09__control_circuits_and_control_functions.md#3. Safety vs. Standard Control Separation]
3. Safety-related software changes shall follow the applicable software lifecycle route, and `IEC 61131-3` alone shall not be treated as a SIL or PL claim basis. [LOCAL: reference_models/software_safety_and_intrinsic_safety_standards.md#PLC Language Standard vs Safety Claim Standard] [LOCAL: reference_models/software_safety_and_intrinsic_safety_standards.md#Safety PLC Software And SIL]
4. Safety communication over a network, if used, shall use a safety-rated black-channel protocol or equivalent certified approach. [LOCAL: international/machinery/iec_60204_1/IEC60204_1_2018__Clause09__control_circuits_and_functions.md#3. Safety vs. Standard Control Separation]
5. Inter-panel signals that remain energized when a local main disconnect is OFF shall be identified as external sources. [LOCAL: us/nfpa79/NFPA79_2024__Ch20__system_integration.md#1. Subsystem Coordination]

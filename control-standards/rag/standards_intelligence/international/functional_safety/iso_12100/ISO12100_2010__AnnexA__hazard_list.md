# ISO 12100:2010 — Annex A — Normative Hazard List

## 0. Why this annex matters

Annex A is normative — it is part of the requirements of the standard, not merely informative guidance. It provides the structured checklist of hazard categories that designers must systematically work through during hazard identification (Clause 4). Using Annex A as a checklist prevents systematic omissions: a hazard not identified cannot be estimated, evaluated, or reduced. Market surveillance authorities and notified bodies use Annex A as a reference to verify that the hazard identification was comprehensive. Every hazard category in Annex A must either be addressed or explicitly ruled out with documented justification in the risk assessment file.

## 1. Mechanical hazards

Mechanical hazards arise from machinery geometry, mass, stiffness, and motion. They are typically the most prevalent hazard category for industrial machinery.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Crushing | Body part caught between converging surfaces | Presses, rollers, clamping fixtures |
| Shearing | Body part caught between two moving edges or between a moving edge and a fixed part | Guillotines, slitters, scissor mechanisms |
| Cutting or severing | Contact with a sharp edge or abrasive surface in motion | Blades, saws, milling cutters, grinding wheels |
| Entanglement | Body part or clothing wound around a rotating element | Shafts, spindles, drills, open chain drives |
| Drawing-in or trapping | Body part drawn into a converging gap | Nip points on gear pairs, belt-and-pulley assemblies, chain drives |
| Impact | Struck by a moving machine element or ejected part | Reciprocating arms, tool ejection, workpiece ejection |
| Stabbing or puncture | Penetration of body tissue by a pointed element | Drill bits, laser heads, pressurised fluid jets |
| Friction or abrasion | Skin contact with fast-moving rough surface | Belt conveyors, grinding wheel periphery |
| Ejection of fluids under high pressure | High-velocity fluid stream penetrating skin | Hydraulic hose failure, pressure wash jets |

## 2. Electrical hazards

Electrical hazards arise from the presence of voltage, current, or stored electrical energy.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Contact with live parts (direct contact) | Skin or tool contact with a conductor at hazardous voltage (> 50 V AC / 120 V DC) | Exposed terminals, damaged cable insulation, open enclosures during maintenance |
| Contact with parts that have become live under fault conditions (indirect contact) | Conductive machine frame energised due to insulation failure | Frame without protective earth; failure of Class I insulation |
| Electrostatic phenomena | Discharge from charged non-conducting surfaces or parts | Powder handling, film processing, solvent spray |
| Thermal effects of electrical origin | Burns from arc flash, overloaded conductors | Short-circuit arcing, overload without adequate protection |

## 3. Thermal hazards

Thermal hazards arise from contact with hot or cold surfaces, materials, or environments, or from exposure to thermal radiation.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Burns and scalds from contact | Skin contact with surfaces or fluids above pain threshold (approx. 48°C for prolonged contact) | Ovens, steam lines, hot dies, coolant |
| Burns from flames or explosions | Direct flame contact or blast wave from ignition of flammable substances | Welding, furnaces, solvent processing |
| Radiation from heat sources | Infrared radiation at levels causing corneal or skin damage | Induction heaters, radiant furnaces, welding arcs |
| Scalds from hot gas or steam | Inhalation or skin contact with hot vapour | Steam traps, autoclaves, boiling process vessels |
| Cold injury (cryogenic or extreme cold) | Contact with cryogenic fluids or extreme cold surfaces | Liquid nitrogen lines, cold store machinery |

## 4. Noise hazards

Noise hazards arise from airborne acoustic energy generated by machinery processes and components.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Hearing loss (noise-induced) | Permanent threshold shift from prolonged or acute exposure to sound pressure levels exceeding 80 dB(A) (action level) or 85 dB(A) (limit level) under Directive 2003/10/EC | Pneumatic exhausts, metal stamping, grinding, woodworking machinery |
| Interference with speech communication | Masking of verbal warnings and communications, increasing error and accident risk | High-ambient-noise production environments |
| Physiological effects | Stress, fatigue, reduced concentration from sustained noise exposure | Any sustained elevated noise environment |

## 5. Vibration hazards

Vibration hazards arise from mechanical oscillation transmitted to the human body through the hands or through the whole body.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Hand-arm vibration (HAV) | Vibration transmitted to hand and arm via a hand-held or hand-guided machine; causes hand-arm vibration syndrome (HAVS) and carpal tunnel syndrome | Grinders, chippers, jackhammers, chainsaw |
| Whole-body vibration (WBV) | Vibration transmitted through a supporting surface to the whole body; causes lumbar spine injury | Ride-on industrial vehicles (forklifts, agricultural machines), vibrating platforms |

## 6. Radiation hazards

Radiation hazards arise from electromagnetic or particulate radiation generated by machinery processes.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Low-frequency electromagnetic fields | Biological effects from sustained exposure to strong fields | Induction heaters, MRI in adjacent areas |
| Radio-frequency (RF) and microwave | Tissue heating from absorbed RF energy | Dielectric welders, microwave ovens, RF sealing machines |
| Infrared and visible optical radiation | Retinal and corneal damage; skin burns | High-power lasers, welding arcs, IR lamps |
| Ultraviolet (UV) radiation | Skin and eye damage (photokeratitis, cataracts) | UV curing equipment, welding, germicidal lamps |
| Ionising radiation | Cellular damage, carcinogenic risk | X-ray inspection systems, radioactive gauges, nuclear instrumentation |
| Laser radiation | Concentrated optical hazard at point of use and from reflections | Laser cutting, marking, welding systems |

## 7. Material and substance hazards

Material and substance hazards arise from contact with, inhalation of, or ingestion of hazardous substances used or produced by the machine.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Contact with or inhalation of harmful fluids, gases, or dusts | Skin absorption, inhalation toxicity, carcinogenic risk | Cutting fluids, hydraulic oils, metal dusts, wood dust, process gases |
| Fire or explosion risk from flammable substances | Ignition of flammable vapours, gases, or dusts | Solvent-based processes, combustible dust environments (ATEX/IECEx) |
| Biological agents | Exposure to pathogenic micro-organisms | Food processing machinery, wastewater equipment, medical device manufacturing |
| Oxygen-depleted atmospheres | Asphyxiation risk from inert gas blanketing or CO2 accumulation | CO2 fire suppression systems, nitrogen-blanketed tanks, enclosed cleaning chambers |

## 8. Ergonomic hazards

Ergonomic hazards arise from the interaction between the operator and the machine when the design does not accommodate human physical and cognitive capabilities.

| Hazard | Description | Typical sources |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Unhealthy posture | Sustained awkward postures leading to musculoskeletal disorders | Controls positioned outside optimal reach; maintenance access requiring sustained bent posture |
| Over-exertion — excessive force | Repetitive or peak force demands exceeding operator capability | Heavy manual tooling; high-force controls; manual workpiece loading |
| Over-exertion — repetitive motion | High-frequency repetitive tasks leading to repetitive strain injury | Assembly line tasks; manual sorting; repetitive triggering of hand tools |
| Cognitive overload | Display information or control arrangements that exceed operator cognitive capacity, leading to errors | Cluttered HMI layouts; poorly organised alarm systems; excessive simultaneous demands |
| Inadequate lighting | Insufficient or inappropriate illumination causing visual fatigue, error, or inability to detect hazards | Maintenance access areas; inspection stations; areas with glare from polished surfaces |
| Thermal comfort | Extreme heat or cold exposure not from direct contact but from the working environment around the machine | Furnace-adjacent workstations; outdoor machinery in extreme climates; cold store machinery |
